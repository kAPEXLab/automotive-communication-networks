What Is an In‑Vehicle Network?

An IVN connects Electronic Control Units (ECUs) through shared communication buses.

  • Reduces wiring complexity
  • Allows data reuse
  • Improves coordination among ECUs
  • Lowers weight and cost

History & Evolution

  • 1980s – CAN introduced
  • 1990s – LIN and MOST emerge
  • 2000s – FlexRay and OBD growth
  • 2010s – CAN FD and Automotive Ethernet
  • 2020s – Zonal Architecture and TSN

E/E Architecture Evolution

Distributed Architecture

Independent ECUs connected with extensive wiring.

Domain-Based Architecture

ECUs grouped under domain controllers.

Zonal Architecture

Zone controllers connected to central high-performance computing units.

Automotive Network Classification

Class Rate Application
A <10 kbit/s Comfort & Body
B 10–125 kbit/s Body Electronics
C 125 kbit/s–1 Mbit/s Powertrain
D >1 Mbit/s Safety & Multimedia

CAN – Controller Area Network

  • Classic CAN: 1 Mbit/s
  • CAN FD: 2–5 Mbit/s
  • Multi-master bus
  • Priority-based arbitration
  • Robust and fault tolerant

LIN – Local Interconnect Network

  • 20 kbit/s
  • Single-wire network
  • One master, multiple slaves
  • Low-cost body electronics

FlexRay

  • 10 Mbit/s per channel
  • Dual-channel redundancy
  • TDMA-based deterministic communication
  • Safety-critical systems

MOST

  • 25–150 Mbit/s
  • Ring topology
  • Audio and multimedia transport

Automotive Ethernet

  • 100 Mbps to 10 Gbps
  • Single Pair Ethernet
  • TSN support
  • ADAS and zonal backbone
  • OTA updates and DoIP

Key Takeaways

  • Networks replaced extensive wiring.
  • No single protocol fits every use case.
  • CAN remains the dominant automotive network.
  • Ethernet is becoming the future backbone.
  • Safety and cybersecurity are essential.
